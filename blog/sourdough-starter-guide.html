Starting Your Sourdough Journey: A Beginner's Guide to Starter

Learn how to create and maintain a sourdough starter, the foundation of amazing homemade bread.

👤 David Chen📅 January 25, 2026⏱️ 9 min read

Sourdough baking has experienced a renaissance, and it all starts with creating and maintaining a healthy sourdough starter. This living culture of wild yeast and bacteria is what gives sourdough its distinctive tangy flavor and chewy texture.

Creating a starter from scratch takes about 5-7 days. You'll need just two ingredients: flour and water. Start with equal parts flour and water (about 1/2 cup each) in a clean jar. Whole wheat or rye flour works faster initially because they contain more wild yeast.

Day 1: Mix flour and water into a thick paste. Cover loosely with a cloth or lid (don't seal it completely - the starter needs air). Leave at room temperature, ideally around 70-75°F.

Days 2-3: You might see some bubbles forming. This is a good sign! Discard half the starter and feed it with fresh flour and water (equal parts). The discard might seem wasteful, but it ensures the right balance of yeast and bacteria.

Days 4-5: The starter should become more active, doubling in size within 4-8 hours of feeding. You'll notice a pleasant, slightly sour smell. Continue the daily feeding routine.

Days 6-7: Your starter should be reliably doubling in size and showing lots of bubbles. It's ready to use when it passes the "float test" - a small spoonful floats in water.

Once established, you can keep your starter at room temperature with daily feedings, or refrigerate it and feed weekly. Room temperature starters are more active but require more attention. Refrigerated starters are lower maintenance but need to be brought to room temperature and fed before baking.

The feeding ratio matters. A typical feeding is 1:1:1 (starter:flour:water), but you can adjust this. A 1:5:5 ratio means less frequent feedings, while 1:1:1 keeps it very active.

What if it develops a liquid layer on top? That's called "hooch" and means your starter is hungry. You can stir it in or pour it off, then feed your starter.

Discard doesn't have to be wasteful. Use it in pancakes, waffles, crackers, or pizza dough. These recipes use the unfed starter and are a delicious way to reduce waste.

Signs of a healthy starter: doubles in size within 4-8 hours of feeding, has a pleasant tangy smell (not vinegary or nail polish-like), shows lots of bubbles, and passes the float test.

Remember, a sourdough starter is forgiving. If you forget to feed it for a few days, it's usually fine. Feed it a couple times and it should bounce back. The more you work with it, the more intuitive it becomes.
